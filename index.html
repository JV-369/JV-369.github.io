If you are here, it is likely that you are interested in finding out whether you are autistic or have some suspicion that you might be. And you would be in great company too! Alan Turing, Greta Thunberg, and Anthony Hopkins are all people who made a huge difference and who have shown the world that autism is not a dirty word and that it is not a disease. Autism is quite literally a state of mind: some people are extroverted, some are gay, and some people are autistic.

Before you take the test

Forget everything you think you know about autism from popular culture. It is almost never like you see in the movies, especially if you are a woman. Very few autistic people are Rain Mans, Sheldon Coopers, or Sherlocks. There is no inherent genius hiding behind every autistic person.

If this test indicates that you might be autistic, do not think of it as a death sentence but an opportunity to explore who you are.

This test was put together by a group of autistic people who post on Instagram as The Autisticats. Unlike most diagnostic tools put together by neurotypical (non-autistic) people, these questions do not focus on how autistic people appear to others and it does not assume you are a four-year old boy interested in train schedules. Instead, it asks about how you see yourself and what your life experiences have been so far. Most importantly, who better screen for autism than other autistic people?

Take the test

Check a box if the answer is yes.

1. Did you learn to speak either very early or unusually late?

2. Did you learn to walk unusually late?

3. When you were younger, did you have "temper tantrums" or outbursts past the age that you were "supposed" to be having them, to the point that it was socially unacceptable?

4. Did you have trouble learning things like tying your shoes and riding a bike when you were a kid?

5. Were you bullied or socially isolated by classmates in your early childhood and elementary years?

6. Were you bullied or socially isolated by your classmates in your middle school and high school years?

7. Do you often wear the same things every day, such as one jacket or one pair of shoes that you wear constantly?

8. Do you often eat the same things every day, or get fixated on certain foods but then get sick of them and move on to something else?

9. Do you get anxious when you don't know what to expect in a given situation, more so than other people?

10. Do you find it difficult to handle loud noises, bright lights, strong smells, and large crowds?

11. Do you find yourself becoming easily disoriented in large open spaces with stairs, like sports arenas and stadiums?

12. Do you often pick up on details in your environment that other people don't notice?

13. Do you pick up on patterns in your environment that other people don't notice?

14. Do you find it difficult and exhausting to make small talk with people you don't know?

15. Do you find it difficult or uncomfortable to make prolonged eye contact with people you don't know well?

16. Do you often do the same things with your body over and over again, like bouncing your leg, fiddling with a pencil, biting your nails, cracking your knuckles, or tapping your fingers on things?

17. Do you have an extreme and unusual passion for a specific subject that you feel compelled to learn everything about, and tell other people about?

18. Do other people often tell you that the way you speak is overly formal, or that you have an unusual affect?

19. Do you often find yourself confused in social situations, unsure of what the "correct" thing to do is?

20. Have you felt like a social outsider your whole life, like you wanted to fit in but never knew how?

21. Have you made friends with autistic people, more easily than you make friends with neurotypical people?

22. Do you get freaked out and thrown off by sudden changes in routine, even if they're minor?

23. Do you find it extremely difficult or impossible to process what people are saying when multiple people are talking to you at the same time?

24. Do you find yourself getting "stuck" on certain thoughts or behaviors, and being unable to let go of them/ stop doing them?

25. Do you find yourself watching other people to observe how they're interacting with each other, then consciously mimicking/mirroring what you see, in order to make sure you're saying/doing things that are "socially appropriate"?

26. Do you often get confused about whether or not someone is joking/being sarcastic, especially if they delivered what they said in a flat tone of voice?

27. Do you have trouble managing & completing everyday tasks like brushing your teeth, showering, doing laundry, etc. without help or reminders?

28. Do you feel like your cognitive/intellectual skills are much higher than your practical, everyday living skills?

29. Have you often been referred to by others as "sensitive," "weird," "strange," or "dramatic"?

30. Do you have habits that others deem inappropriate for your age?

What's Next?

If you answered yes to very few questions you are most likely neurotypical. Having said that, no test is perfect and this one is only indicative, so if you still suspect you might be autistic donâ€™t let this stop you.

If you answered yes to 15 or more questions you can talk to a psychiatrist or another health care professional and ask about your options for being evaluated for autism.

However, an official diagnosis is not necessary to be and call yourself autistic. In fact, some people consciously make the decision not to pursue an evaluation because it changes nothing about their lives. That is perfectly fine. Some people, unfortunately, would like to talk to a professional and get a diagnosis, but (especially if you live in the US) this can be prohibitively expensive or there are no professionals who diagnose autism in adults locally. If this is your case, you can absolutely consider yourself to be autistic even without an official diagnosis.

If you want to seek a diagnosis the resources section directs you to a list of professionals who can diagnose autism in adults, but it is also a good idea to look locally for someone who might not be on the list.

You can also try connecting with other autistic people on social media (links in the resources section) to learn about autism.
